# UX Laws Catalog

This catalog contains 30 principles of User Experience (UX) design, often referred to as the Laws of UX.

## 1. Aesthetic-Usability Effect
- **Definition:** Users often perceive aesthetically pleasing design as design that’s more usable.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - An aesthetically pleasing design creates a positive response in people’s brains and leads them to believe the design actually works better.
    - People are more tolerant of minor usability issues when the design of a product or service is aesthetically pleasing.
    - Visually pleasing design can mask usability problems and prevent issues from being discovered during usability testing.
- **Origins:** First studied in 1995 by Masaaki Kurosu and Kaori Kashimura at the Hitachi Design Center.

## 2. Choice Overload
- **Definition:** The tendency for people to get overwhelmed when they are presented with a large number of options.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Too many options hurts users’ decision-making ability.
    - Enable side-by-side comparison of related items and options.
    - Prioritize content and provide tools for narrowing down choices (search, filtering).
- **Origins:** Introduced by Alvin Toffler in 1970 (*Future Shock*).

## 3. Chunking
- **Definition:** Breaking down individual pieces of information into smaller, meaningful groups.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Chunking enables users to easily scan content.
    - Structure content into visually distinct groups with clear hierarchy.
    - Helps users understand underlying relationships by grouping content into distinctive modules.
- **Origins:** Derived from George A. Miller's 1956 paper on short-term memory limits (7 ± 2).

## 4. Cognitive Bias
- **Definition:** Systematic errors in thinking that influence perception and decision-making.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - We use heuristics (rules of thumb) based on past experiences to conserve mental energy.
    - Understanding biases helps identify and safeguard against costly mistakes.
    - Confirmation bias is a common example (seeking info that confirms existing beliefs).
- **Origins:** Introduced by Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman in 1972.

## 5. Cognitive Load
- **Definition:** The amount of mental resources needed to understand and interact with an interface.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - When info exceeds mental space, tasks become harder and details are missed.
    - Intrinsic load: Effort to absorb relevant info.
    - Extraneous load: Mental processing that doesn't help understand content (distracting design).
- **Origins:** Developed in the late 1980s by John Sweller.

## 6. Doherty Threshold
- **Definition:** Productivity soars when interaction occurs at a pace (<400ms) where neither human nor computer has to wait.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Provide system feedback within 400ms to keep user attention.
    - Use perceived performance (animations, progress bars) to improve response time perception.
    - Purposeful delays can sometimes increase perceived value and trust.
- **Origins:** Published in 1982 by Walter J. Doherty and Ahrvind J. Thadani.

## 7. Fitts’s Law
- **Definition:** The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Touch targets should be large enough and have ample spacing.
    - Place targets in areas that are easily acquired.
- **Origins:** Proposed by psychologist Paul Fitts in 1954.

## 8. Flow
- **Definition:** A mental state of full immersion and energized focus in an activity.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Occurs when there's a balance between task difficulty and user skill.
    - Provide necessary feedback so users know what's accomplished.
    - Remove unnecessary friction.
- **Origins:** Coined by Mihály Csíkszentmihályi in 1975.

## 9. Goal-Gradient Effect
- **Definition:** The tendency to approach a goal increases with proximity to that goal.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Users work faster as they get closer to completion.
    - Providing artificial progress helps maintain motivation.
    - Provide clear indications of progress.
- **Origins:** Proposed by Clark Hull in 1932.

## 10. Hick’s Law
- **Definition:** The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Minimize choices when response times are critical.
    - Break complex tasks into smaller steps.
    - Highlight recommended options.
- **Origins:** Named after William Edmund Hick and Ray Hyman (1952).

## 11. Jakob's Law
- **Definition:** Users spend most of their time on other sites, so they prefer your site to work like others they know.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Users transfer expectations from familiar products to similar ones.
    - Leveraging existing mental models allows users to focus on tasks.
    - Minimize discord when making changes by allowing access to familiar versions temporarily.
- **Origins:** Coined by Jakob Nielsen.

## 12. Law of Common Region
- **Definition:** Elements in an area with a clearly defined boundary are perceived as a group.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Creates clear structure and helps users understand relationships.
    - Use borders or background colors to define regions.
- **Origins:** Gestalt law of grouping.

## 13. Law of Proximity
- **Definition:** Objects near each other tend to be grouped together.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Establishes relationships with nearby objects.
    - Elements close together are perceived to share traits/functionality.
- **Origins:** Gestalt law of grouping.

## 14. Law of Prägnanz
- **Definition:** People interpret complex images in the simplest form possible.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Reduces cognitive effort.
    - Fundamental to how we organize patterns.
- **Origins:** Fundamental principle of Gestalt psychology.

## 15. Law of Similarity
- **Definition:** The eye perceives similar elements as a complete picture or group.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Visually similar elements (color, shape, size) are perceived as related.
    - Differentiate links and navigation from normal text.
- **Origins:** Gestalt law of grouping.

## 16. Law of Uniform Connectedness
- **Definition:** Visually connected elements are perceived as more related.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Group similar functions with lines, frames, or shapes.
    - Use connecting references (arrows, lines) to show context.
- **Origins:** Gestalt law of grouping.

## 17. Mental Model
- **Definition:** What a user believes they know about how a system works.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Users apply models from past experiences to new, similar situations.
    - Align design with users' mental models to improve experience.
- **Origins:** Kenneth Craik (1943).

## 18. Miller's Law
- **Definition:** The average person can keep 7 (± 2) items in their working memory.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Organize content into smaller chunks.
    - Capacity varies based on prior knowledge and context.
- **Origins:** George Miller (1956).

## 19. Occam's Razor
- **Definition:** Among competing hypotheses, the one with the fewest assumptions is usually best.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Best way to reduce complexity is to avoid it.
    - Remove elements without compromising function.
- **Origins:** William of Ockham (14th century).

## 20. Paradox of the Active User
- **Definition:** Users often start using software immediately instead of reading manuals.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Users are motivated by immediate tasks.
    - Provide accessible, contextual guidance (tooltips).
- **Origins:** Rosson and Carroll (1987).

## 21. Pareto Principle
- **Definition:** Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Inputs/outputs are not evenly distributed.
    - Focus effort on areas with largest benefits for most users.
- **Origins:** Vilfredo Pareto (economist).

## 22. Parkinson’s Law
- **Definition:** Tasks expand to fill the available time.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Limit time for tasks to match user expectations.
    - Use features like autofill to save time and prevent task inflation.
- **Origins:** Cyril Northcote Parkinson (1955).

## 23. Peak-End Rule
- **Definition:** People judge an experience based on its peak and its end.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Focus on the most intense and final moments of a journey.
    - Negative experiences are recalled more vividly.
- **Origins:** Kahneman et al. (1993).

## 24. Postel’s Law
- **Definition:** Be liberal in what you accept, and conservative in what you send.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Be tolerant of various user inputs/actions.
    - Anticipate virtually anything in terms of input while providing reliable output.
- **Origins:** Jon Postel (Internet pioneer).

## 25. Selective Attention
- **Definition:** Focusing only on stimuli related to current goals.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Users filter out irrelevant info.
    - Banner Blindness: Users ignore ad-like content.
    - Change Blindness: Significant changes can go unnoticed.
- **Origins:** Donald Broadbent, Anne Treisman.

## 26. Serial Position Effect
- **Definition:** Users best remember the first and last items in a series.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Place least important items in the middle.
    - Position key actions on the far left or right (primacy/recency effects).
- **Origins:** Herman Ebbinghaus.

## 27. Tesler’s Law
- **Definition:** Every system has an inherent complexity that cannot be reduced.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Complexity must be assumed by either system or user.
    - Designers should assume the burden of complexity.
- **Origins:** Larry Tesler (Xerox PARC).

## 28. Von Restorff Effect
- **Definition:** Distinctive items among similar ones are most likely to be remembered.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Make important info visually distinctive.
    - Don't rely solely on color for contrast.
- **Origins:** Hedwig von Restorff (1933).

## 29. Working Memory
- **Definition:** Temporarily holds info needed for tasks.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Capacity is 4-7 chunks, lasting 20-30 seconds.
    - Support recognition over recall (visited links, breadcrumbs).
- **Origins:** Miller, Galanter, Pribram (1960s).

## 30. Zeigarnik Effect
- **Definition:** People remember uncompleted tasks better than completed ones.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - Invite discovery by showing signifiers of more content.
    - Artificial progress helps maintain motivation.
- **Origins:** Bluma Zeigarnik (1920s).
